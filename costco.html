01 / Executive Summary

Three valuation methods, one consistent signal.

All three intrinsic value approaches — NOPAT-based DCF, an exit-multiple valuation using UFCF and historical EV/EBITDA, and a forward P/E target — produce a per-share value below the prevailing market price. The base case implies Costco trades at a meaningful premium to the cash flows our forecasts support. Sensitivity testing on WACC and terminal growth confirms the signal: only at sub-7% WACC combined with terminal growth above 5.5% does the intrinsic value approach market price.

Take-away: The market price of $1,011 implies the consensus is pricing in either (a) a substantially lower discount rate than CAPM justifies, (b) a terminal growth rate well above US GDP, or (c) a sustained re-rating of the Costco P/E multiple beyond the defensive-retail peer set. Our base case supports a cautious / SELL stance pending one of those three conditions changing.
02 / Company Snapshot

Footprint, mix & the membership flywheel.

Costco operates 914 warehouses today, projected to reach 942 by end of FY26 with a clear US-led expansion bias. Net sales remain dominated by Foods & Sundries (~41%) and Non-Foods (~26%), with Fresh Foods (~14%) compounding fastest. Membership fees — $5.3B in FY25 — flow nearly 100% to operating income and form the structural margin moat.

05 / Beta Calculation (CAPM)

60-month regression of excess returns.

Monthly returns for COST and the S&P 500 over the trailing five years (Mar-2021 to Feb-2026) are converted to excess-of-Rf returns using the 3-month T-bill yield. Ordinary least squares regression of (Re − Rf) on (Rm − Rf) yields the CAPM beta. The fitted slope of 1.015 indicates Costco moves roughly one-for-one with the market — consistent with its defensive-retail classification — with an intercept of +0.9% (statistically insignificant).

06 / Weighted Average Cost of Capital

Cost of equity, cost of debt, and the 9.43% blend.

07 / DCF Valuation

From free cash flow to intrinsic value.

The NOPAT method builds free cash flow from operating income net of taxes less the period change in net operating assets (NOA = operating assets − operating liabilities), terminating with a Gordon-growth perpetuity at g = 3.5%. The exit-multiple method takes the conventional UFCF buildup — EBIT × (1−t) + D&A − CapEx − ΔWC — and sets the terminal value at the 5-year historical EV/EBITDA multiple of 27.8× applied to terminal-year EBITDA. Both discount at the 9.43% WACC over the explicit 3-year horizon (FY26E–FY28E).

08 / Working Capital & Additional Financing Needs

Operating-cycle efficiency & surplus capacity.

Costco's cash conversion cycle of 1.80 days is structurally superior to peers — by collecting from members upfront and rapidly turning inventory, the business funds growth from float. Applying the AFN formula across the forecast horizon shows Costco generates a financing surplus in every year — meaning operating cash flow more than covers the asset growth required to support sales. This surplus could fund special dividends of $4.98–$10.75 per share annually beyond the regular payout (FY28 jumps as growth normalizes to the 3.5% terminal rate).

Cash Conversion Cycle — 2024 Peer Comparison
Costco's CCC of 2.7 days vs Target's 12.3 — driven by industry-leading inventory turnover (12.6x vs Target ~6x) and a payables deferral period that matches Walmart's. Negative CCC at Amazon reflects its very different business model.

09 / Recommendation
Investment View · Base Case · As of February 2026

Trading at a premium to intrinsic value — cautious / SELL.

All three valuation lenses point to Costco trading above the cash-flow-supported value at the current market price of $1,011. The NOPAT DCF (Gordon-growth terminal at 3.5%) anchors at $302; the exit-multiple method (UFCF discounted at WACC + EBITDA × 27.8× historical multiple) reaches $819; the forward P/E method on industry comps lands at $590. Bridging this gap requires assumptions outside our base set — a sub-7.5% WACC, terminal growth above 5.5%, or a sustained multiple expansion beyond the defensive-retail peer set.

  • Operational quality is real and unchanged. 942 stores by FY26E, 28% ROE, sub-2-day cash conversion cycle, and a membership-fee moat that flows ~100% to operating income.
  • The mismatch is valuation, not fundamentals. Even bull-case FCFF growth and a generous terminal multiple don't close the gap to $1,011 unless WACC compresses materially.
  • AFN surplus signals optionality. $9.3B of cumulative surplus through FY28E supports special dividends or buyback acceleration — a near-term catalyst for total-return holders.
  • Beta is robust but R² is moderate. Slope of 1.015 (t-stat 6.76) is highly significant, but 0.44 R² means ~56% of return variance is idiosyncratic — a reminder that single-factor CAPM doesn't capture the membership-flywheel premium investors are paying for.
  • Watch list of upside catalysts. A 50bp drop in 10Y yields (lifting our terminal-growth tolerance), a margin step-change from private-label mix, or an international acceleration could shift the base case toward $400+.
  • Downside protection is structural. Defensive retail exposure, beta ≈ 1, and a fortress balance sheet (net cash of $5.3B) limit drawdown risk even if multiple compression materializes.
This view is anchored to Feb 2026 market data — COST $1,011.17, Rf 4.02%, ERP 5.5%, β 1.0145. Re-running the model on later prices, rates, or earnings revisions can shift the call materially. Sensitivity grid in section 07 shows the range.
